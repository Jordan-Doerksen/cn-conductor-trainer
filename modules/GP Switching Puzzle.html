Switching Puzzle — Dig Out the Cut
Plan your moves, then run · tie on, pull, kick, shove · fewest trips and fewest joints

Your plan

Yard map — where the tracks are
Gold = set-out track · blue = inbound · grey = scratch · orange dots = switches. Engine works the throat (left) end. ⚡kick = a track you can kick into on the end you're working.

How to play

You're the conductor on the ground. The engine 🚂 works the throat — the left (⟵) end of every track. Get the right cars onto the gold track and leave the engine empty, in as few trips — and as few couplings — as you can.

1 · Read the work order

The yellow bar up top is your switch list. It says which cars to set out (or, on a build, the exact order to make) and on which track. Par is the move count a tight line — no wasted trips — would take.

2 · Your moves

PULL nTie on and pull the n cars nearest the throat out onto the lead. They ride on the engine. Tying on is a joint.
SPOT nShove the n cars at the engine’s working end into a track and cut them off. Onto cars already there you couple — that’s a joint. Spotting 2+ cars secures the cut — you set a hand brake before you cut away — so you can then kick onto it.
KICK nCut the n cars off rolling so they coast in on their own — no joint. You can only kick onto a secured standing cut of 2+ cars on a north-end ⚡kick track — never an empty or loose track. (Spotting 2+ cars secures it; pulling from a cut releases it.)

Think of the engine as a stack: the last cars you PULL sit at the working end, so they're the first you can SPOT or KICK. The order you lift cars in is the whole game.

Which end you're building

The work order tags each job NORTH end or SOUTH end — it changes where you can kick. North end (most builds, when you can): kick into the grade tracks marked ⚡kick. South end: you can only kick into AS71, and the GP special instruction is a minimum of 2 hand brakes on to do it.

Full yards — cars already standing

On the Full yard puzzles the scratch tracks aren't empty — cuts are already standing, and some arrive already tied down (🔒). That flips the job: instead of spotting and securing a cut yourself, you read the yard — which cut is tied (kick onto it free), which has room, which is full and fouling — and route your spares accordingly. This is the realistic case, and the hardest tier; learn the moves on the emptier tracks first.

Two scores — moves & joints

Moves are trips; par is the tightest trip count. Joints are couplings — every tie-on, every shove onto standing cars. Kicking is zero joints — but to kick you first have to spot a cut onto a kick track (which secures it), and that spot still costs a trip. So kicking only wins when there’s enough volume to make it worth the setup: on a big cut, secure a track and kick the rest; on a small one, just spot it. That judgment — is the setup worth it? — is the real call, and the score teaches it.

3 · Build a plan, then run it

  1. Pick PULL, SPOT, KICK, set the count, choose the track, hit Add move.
  2. Stack up the full plan — it lists under Your plan.
  3. Hit Run plan to watch it work down the yard. Reset starts over.
  4. Stuck? Show a tight (par) solution reveals one clean line, and after a win you can watch the low-joint kick line.

Worked example — puzzle #1

Order: set out B to AS73. AS71 holds A‑B‑C (A nearest the throat).

1. PULL 2 from AS71  → engine holds A‑B, C left behind  (+1 joint)
2. SPOT 1 to AS73  → B onto the empty gold track ✓  (no joint)
3. SPOT 1 to AS72  → A parked, engine clear  (no joint)

Three moves, par 3, one joint. No kicking here — there’s no secured cut to kick onto, and you never kick into an empty track. On a bigger cut you’d spot a couple cars to a ⚡kick track — that secures it — then kick the spares onto it.
