How a signal name reads

Block and interlocking signals (CROR 405–440) aren't random names — there's a grammar. Learn it and you can decode a signal instead of memorising a picture.

The rule: speed-here → speed-at-next

A two-part name reads left = how you pass THIS signal, right = what to expect at the NEXT signal.

Medium to Slow → MEDIUM speed past this signal & turnouts, then be ready for SLOW at the next one.
Clear to Stop → proceed (Clear) here, but prepare to stop at the next signal.
Limited to Clear → LIMITED speed through here, then it opens up.

The ones that stand alone

Clear (405) — proceed. Restricting (436) — restricted speed. Stop and Proceed (437) — stop, then restricted speed. Stop (439) — stop. Diverging (430) — reduced speed, not over diverging speed through the turnouts.

"Advance" means look further

Advance Clear to Stop (415) reaches two signals out — proceed now, but be prepared to stop at the second signal.

Controlled vs non-controlled — single vs staggered red

A controlled signal is one the RTC can hold at Stop (CROR: “capable of displaying a Stop indication until requested to display a less restrictive indication by the RTC”), at a controlled location or point. Its red is an absolute Stop (439) — a single red. A non-controlled (automatic / intermediate) signal can’t show absolute Stop; its red is Stop and Proceed (437) — two reds, staggered. The stagger is the tell: it’s permissive, so you may stop and then proceed at restricted speed.

Plaques on the mast

A plate can qualify a signal (CROR 403(b)). The ones the rules name: a DV plate is required on Diverging indications, and a Restricting Signal can carry an R plate (Rule 573). There is no “A” (absolute) plate in Canada — that's US practice.
